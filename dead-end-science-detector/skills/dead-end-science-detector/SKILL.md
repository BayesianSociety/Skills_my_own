# Dead-End Science Detector

Use this skill to evaluate whether a scientific direction, technology, or public-company technology narrative is
healthy, immature but promising, niche, commercially blocked, narrative-driven, or likely to be a dead end.

The goal is not to dismiss risky science. The goal is to test whether a research programme is becoming more
predictive, reproducible, scalable, economically viable, and useful over time.

Do not hallucinate. Do not label a field or company technology dead merely because it is early. If evidence is
insufficient, say so.

## Core Definition

A scientific or technological direction is a likely dead end if it repeatedly fails to move from:

claim -> evidence -> replication -> mechanism -> scale-up -> practical consequence

Distinguish two types of dead end:

- **Epistemic dead end**: the science itself is weak, non-reproducible, non-predictive, unfalsifiable, or too
flexible to test.
- **Translational dead end**: the science is real, but cannot become useful because of cost, safety, regulation,
manufacturability, infrastructure, adoption, or incumbent alternatives.

A field can be scientifically valid but commercially dead.

## Workflow

### 1. Define The Claim Precisely

Convert vague claims into testable claims.

Ask:

- What exactly is being claimed?
- What measurable outcome should improve?
- Compared with what current alternative?
- Under what conditions?
- By when?
- At what cost, risk, or complexity?
- What result would weaken or falsify the claim?

If the claim cannot be made testable, flag this as a major warning sign.

Use the logic of the Heilmeier questions:

- What is the objective in plain language?
- How is it done today?
- What are the limits of current practice?
- What is new?
- Who cares?
- What are the risks?
- How much will it cost?
- How long will it take?
- What are the mid-term and final tests?

### 2. Separate Science Risk From Application Risk

Evaluate scientific validity and practical viability separately.

Science-risk questions:

- Is the effect real?
- Is the mechanism plausible?
- Can independent groups reproduce it?
- Does it make risky predictions?
- Does explanatory power improve over time?

Application-risk questions:

- Can it be manufactured, deployed, regulated, maintained, or paid for?
- Does it beat existing solutions?
- Does it scale outside ideal laboratory conditions?
- Are users, firms, regulators, or customers willing to adopt it?

Use technology readiness and adoption readiness frameworks where relevant. Technical feasibility is not the same
as adoption feasibility.

### 3. Build A Research-Programme Timeline

Do not judge one paper. Judge the trajectory over time.

For the last 5, 10, or 20 years, ask:

- Has the field moved from theory to experiment?
- From experiment to independent replication?
- From replication to larger systems?
- From prototypes to real-world tests?
- From real-world tests to adoption?
- Or is it repeatedly publishing early-stage "promising" results?

A field is healthier when each generation of work increases predictive power, empirical support, and practical
maturity.

A field is more suspect when publication volume rises but maturity does not.

### 4. Test Reproducibility And Replicability

Check whether the evidence can be reproduced and replicated.

- **Reproducibility**: can others obtain the same result using the same data, code, and methods?
- **Replicability**: can others obtain consistent results using new data, new experiments, or independent
samples?

Ask:

- Are data available?
- Is code available?
- Are materials and protocols available?
- Are methods described in enough detail?
- Have independent groups replicated the finding?
- Are replications from competitors, neutral groups, or only insiders?
- Do effect sizes shrink over time?

Weak reproducibility does not automatically prove a dead end, but persistent non-replication is a major warning
sign.

### 5. Audit Study Quality

For each important paper or evidence claim, examine:

- sample size
- controls
- randomization where applicable
- blinding where applicable
- statistical power
- multiple-testing correction
- preregistration where relevant
- endpoint quality
- data availability
- code availability
- conflict of interest
- funding source
- whether the result is a proxy or a meaningful outcome

Be especially skeptical of small, flexible, underpowered, highly selective, or non-transparent studies.

### 6. Look For Repeated Bottlenecks

Create a timeline of unresolved obstacles.

Common bottlenecks include:

- poor reproducibility
- weak effect size
- unstable materials
- toxicity
- safety risk
- excessive energy use
- poor manufacturability
- inability to scale
- high cost
- regulatory infeasibility
- lack of adoption
- scarce inputs
- fragile supply chain
- weak benchmark advantage
- failure under real-world heterogeneity

If the same barrier appears for many years and is always deferred to "future work," raise the dead-end risk.

### 7. Compare Against The Incumbent

A new science competes against the future incumbent, not today's incumbent.

Ask:

- How fast is the incumbent improving?
- Is the new approach marginally better or dramatically better?
- Does it require new infrastructure?
- Does it introduce new reliability, safety, cost, or complexity problems?
- Would customers, clinicians, regulators, firms, or users switch?
- Is the switching cost justified?

Many technologies fail because the existing solution improves faster than the new one matures.

### 8. Demand Risky Predictions

A healthy research programme should become more predictive.

Ask:

- What would convince supporters they are wrong?
- Has the theory predicted something unexpected?
- Did later evidence confirm that prediction?
- Are negative results incorporated honestly?
- Or does the explanation change after every failure?

A theory that can explain every possible result after the fact is weak.

Do not treat one failed experiment as decisive. Look for whether the field learns from failure or only protects
itself from failure.

### 9. Check External Validation

Positive signs:

- independent replications
- outside-lab adoption
- industry pilots
- regulatory engagement
- standards activity
- procurement interest
- insurance or financing models
- open benchmarks
- shared datasets
- credible negative-result literature

Negative signs:

- only founder-lab results
- only insider citations
- only conference hype
- no independent replication
- patents with no prototypes
- startups with no technical disclosure
- repeated review papers but few working demonstrations

### 10. Run A Scale-Up Audit

Identify what must scale for the science to matter.

Depending on the field, assess:

- materials supply
- manufacturing yield
- safety
- reliability
- data infrastructure
- workforce skills
- regulatory pathway
- distribution
- maintenance
- energy use
- environmental impact
- cost curve
- customer behavior
- real-world operating conditions

A scientific result can be true and still fail at scale.

### 11. Use Bibliometrics Carefully

Use literature-indexing and citation tools to map the field, not to prove validity.

Current tools may include OpenAlex, Semantic Scholar, Crossref, Lens, Dimensions, Web of Science, Scopus,
PubMed, arXiv, SSRN, TechRxiv, and institutional repositories.

Look for:

- publication growth
- citation concentration
- leading institutions entering or leaving
- independent groups contributing
- movement from theory to experiment to deployment
- whether citations come from research papers or mostly reviews
- whether industry, regulators, or standards bodies are engaging

Publication growth alone is not evidence of progress.

### 12. Check Patents, Standards, Funding, And Capital Allocation

Use these as supporting evidence.

Positive signs:

- patents tied to concrete implementation problems
- standards development
- tool or infrastructure investment
- regulatory pathway work
- pilot deployments
- procurement programs
- capital expenditure
- customer qualification
- production or deployment milestones

Negative signs:

- broad conceptual patents only
- no standards activity
- no implementation ecosystem
- no customer qualification
- no serious funding beyond early research
- no capital allocation despite years of claims

Patents are weak evidence by themselves. Standards and customer adoption are stronger.

### 13. Public-Company Technology Risk Layer

Use this section when evaluating whether a publicly traded company is pursuing or promoting a technology that
may be a scientific, technical, or commercial dead end.

Prioritize regulated and primary sources:

- annual reports
- Securities and Exchange Commission filings
- 10-K, 10-Q, 20-F, 6-K, S-1, F-1, 8-K filings
- earnings call transcripts
- investor presentations
- audited financial statements
- management discussion and analysis
- risk factors
- capital expenditure disclosures
- segment reporting
- customer concentration disclosures
- backlog and order disclosures
- impairment charges
- restructuring announcements
- regulatory filings
- product certification documents
- patent filings
- standards-body participation
- litigation records where relevant

Treat press releases, interviews, conference talks, and promotional videos as weaker evidence unless confirmed
by filings, revenue, customers, or technical documentation.

Company-specific questions:

- Does the company disclose revenue from the technology, or only future potential?
- Is the technology mentioned in audited or regulated filings?
- Are there named customers, purchase commitments, or only pilots?
- Is there evidence of repeat sales?
- Are margins improving or worsening as deployment scales?
- Is capital expenditure aligned with the claimed technology roadmap?
- Are delays, impairments, write-downs, or restructuring charges appearing?
- Do risk factors repeatedly mention unresolved technical, regulatory, manufacturing, or supply-chain barriers?
- Are timelines repeatedly pushed forward?
- Are performance metrics independently verified?
- Does the company change terminology when milestones are missed?
- Are related-party customers or non-binding memoranda being presented as demand?
- Is the company dependent on subsidies, grants, tax credits, or one-off contracts?
- Does the technology beat incumbent alternatives on cost, reliability, performance, safety, and adoption?

Public-company red flags:

- large claims but no material revenue
- repeated delays in commercialization
- vague or changing key performance indicators
- pilots that do not convert into repeat customers
- heavy capital spending without yield, output, or margin improvement
- revenue recognized from prototypes rather than scalable products
- frequent equity issuance to fund continuing losses
- dependence on government subsidies without private-market demand
- impairments or write-downs of technology assets
- customer cancellations or shrinking backlog
- auditor going-concern warnings
- management turnover in technical or operations roles
- risk factors that repeat the same unresolved bottlenecks year after year
- promotional language stronger than regulated disclosure
- patents without production evidence
- no credible comparison against incumbent technologies

### 14. Set Kill Criteria

Before judging further investment or belief, define what must happen next.

Examples:

- independent replication by unaffiliated groups
- effect size above a predefined threshold
- cost below a benchmark
- successful field trial
- acceptable safety profile
- manufacturing yield above a threshold
- clear regulatory pathway
- real-world performance superior to incumbent
- adoption by credible users or customers
- disclosure of material revenue or repeat customers for public companies

If the field or company repeatedly misses kill criteria and moves the goalposts, classify it as degenerating or
high risk.

## Classification Framework

Use categories rather than false precision.

Scientific and technological classifications:

- **Healthy science**: reproducible, predictive, improving, externally validated, and moving up the maturity
ladder.
- **Immature but promising**: early evidence, clear tests, known obstacles, and credible path forward.
- **High-risk science**: interesting evidence, but weak replication, unclear mechanism, or unresolved scale-up
barriers.
- **Likely niche**: real effect, but limited use cases or weak general-purpose impact.
- **Translational dead end**: scientifically real, but blocked by cost, adoption, regulation, manufacturability,
safety, or incumbents.
- **Epistemic dead end**: weak, non-reproducible, non-predictive, unfalsifiable, or dominated by post-hoc
explanations.
- **Too early to judge**: evidence is insufficient for a strong conclusion.

Public-company technology classifications:

- **Commercially validated**: meaningful revenue, repeat customers, improving margins, and independent
validation.
- **Strategic but unproven**: credible investment and technical progress, but limited revenue or adoption.
- **Pilot-stage risk**: prototypes or demonstrations exist, but scale-up and customer conversion are weak.
- **Narrative-driven**: company valuation or messaging depends heavily on future claims not yet supported by
filings.
- **Likely dead-end exposure**: repeated technical or commercial failure, weak adoption, deteriorating
economics, and no credible path to scale.
- **Insufficient evidence**: public information is not enough to judge.

The "likely niche" category is important. Many technologies are not fake or useless; they are simply narrower
than advertised.

## Red Flags

A field or company technology narrative is drifting toward dead-end status if many of these are present:

- vague claims
- no clear falsification test
- small or underpowered studies
- weak independent replication
- shrinking effect sizes
- repeated unresolved bottlenecks
- no movement up readiness levels
- no external validation
- no cost or scale-up path
- no advantage against incumbents
- dependence on hype language
- insiders mostly cite each other
- failures are explained away rather than used to improve the theory
- publication growth without maturity growth
- public-company promotion without regulated disclosure support
- patents without production evidence
- pilots without repeat customers
- repeated commercialization delays

## Evidence Discipline

For each important claim, distinguish:

- confirmed fact
- scientific result
- replication evidence
- company or institutional claim
- regulated public-company disclosure
- customer commitment
- revenue evidence
- margin evidence
- patent signal
- market interpretation
- uncertainty

Never present:

- publication volume as proof of truth
- patents as proof of adoption
- preprints as peer-reviewed evidence
- company marketing as proof of technical success
- early prototypes as proof of scalability
- pilots as proof of repeatable demand
- non-binding memoranda as firm customer commitments

Do not call a company fraudulent unless there is strong legal or evidentiary basis. Prefer precise language such
as "commercialization risk is high," "evidence of scale-up is weak," "the technology remains unvalidated in
public disclosures," or "the public evidence does not support the claimed maturity."

## Recommended Output Structure

When producing an assessment, include:

1. Claim being evaluated
2. Current alternative or incumbent
3. Evidence base and source quality
4. Research-programme timeline
5. Reproducibility and replication status
6. Mechanism and predictive power
7. Repeated bottlenecks
8. Scale-up and adoption audit
9. Incumbent comparison
10. External validation
11. Public-company disclosure analysis, if applicable
12. Capital allocation and revenue evidence, if applicable
13. Kill criteria
14. Classification
15. Confidence level
16. Explicit uncertainties

## Core Test

A scientific or technological direction is probably a dead end when it keeps producing publications, narratives,
patents, pilots, and promises, but does not produce increasingly reliable predictions, independent replications,
scale-up evidence, material adoption, or practical consequences.
